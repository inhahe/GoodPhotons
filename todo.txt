"1. Does the renderer convert isosurfaces to meshes before rendering? No.

I should correct the impression I gave. At render time the renderer intersects an isosurface directly by sphere-tracing / ray-marching the analytic field inside its container (intersectImplicit, render_cuda.cu:1223) — it never builds a mesh. Marching cubes is used only offline, by two commands:
-export-mesh (write an OBJ), and
-check-watertight (polygonise the field just so it can reuse the same mesh edge-manifold checker).

The practical consequence: an isosurface's real airtightness at render time is a property of the field's zero level-set and its container caps, not of any mesh. A truly closed level-set is inherently airtight when marched directly; the render-time leak risks are instead (a) a contained_by box clipping the surface into an open cap, (b) march overshoot from a wrong Lipschitz/max-gradient bound, (c) features thinner than the step. So -check-watertight on an isosurface is a proxy — it audits the polygonised copy, which is a very good indicator but not literally the geometry the renderer marches."

- (A) Pure-forward catch. Photon physically lands on the film surface → accumulate → terminate. Dead simple, maximally honest, GPU-perfect. Just slow, and slower the smaller the aperture. Good for "show me the physical lens doing lens things.
(B) Connect-to-pupil splatting (light tracing with next-event estimation to the camera). At every photon bounce, additionally: sample a point on the camera's entrance pupil, form the connection ray from bounce→pupil, and if unobstructed, splat a weighted contribution onto the film. This is the standard fix and it's orders of magnitude faster because every bounce contributes to the image instead of only the rare photon that happens to hit. - not maximally honest
The subtlety — and it's important given your physical-lens goal: you cannot connect a diffuse bounce through the lens glass, because refractive lens elements are specular (delta BSDF) and the connection probability is zero (this is the SDS problem again). The fix: connect to the entrance pupil (the front of the lens system), and once you have a position+direction hitting the pupil, the path through the lens elements is deterministic (specular refraction is deterministic given a ray) — trace it through the elements, and if it lands on the film, splat. So (B) works even with a full physical multi-element lens; you're just importance-sampling the pupil instead of praying a photon flies into it. Crucially, each photon is still independent → still GPU-friendly.

Isosurfaces — the general case: any scalar field f(x,y,z), surface = {p : f(p)=0}. Metaballs, gyroids, tori, hearts, noise-perturbed spheres all fall out of the function. Two ways to intersect a ray:
Sphere-tracing an SDF (signed distance function): step by the field value, guaranteed not to overshoot. Fast, robust, but the field must be a true distance (or Lipschitz-bounded).
Root-finding a general f along the ray: sample f at intervals, bracket a sign change, bisect/regula-falsi. Handles arbitrary f (POV-Ray's approach) but needs a max_gradient/step budget to not miss thin features.
