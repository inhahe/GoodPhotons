claude:

This is a rich pile of ideas, and buried in it is a single organizing principle that answers most of your hard questions at once. Let me pull that out first, then hit the specifics.
The one principle: keep everything as functions/fields; discretize last, per frame

Almost every "how do I make this work over time" snag you hit is the same snag: you're trying to transport a discretization (sampled points, a mesh, a frame) through time. Don't. Animate the continuous thing and re-sample it every frame.
Your gyroid-rotated-in-N-D contiguity problem dissolves completely. Don't rotate the gyroid's points — rotate the domain of the field. A gyroid is F(x,y,z)=0. An N-D rotation is just a coordinate transform p' = R(t)·embed(p); you evaluate F(project(p')) and re-extract the isosurface each frame with your existing marching/root-finder. Contiguity is guaranteed by construction because you re-solve the surface every frame — there are no "points" to keep in order.
Same answer for N-D control grids and N-D functions: a slice of a smooth field is a smooth field. Mesh the slice, per frame.
Same answer for modulated isosurface parameters: change the parameter, re-mesh.

So the render-cost worry is real but bounded to exactly one thing — final path-traced frames — and never touches authoring. Which leads to the worth-it question:
Is it worth it? Yes, but decouple the two layers

The authoring/geometry layer is renderer-independent and the durable, reusable asset. It emits time-parametrized geometry/fields. Good Photons is just one consumer; a raster/GL preview is another (you already have a raster z-buffer path). So:
Iterate against the cheap preview. 99% of animation work is timing and shape iteration — do that at 60fps in the raster path. Only commit to path-traced frames for finals. The slowness of "real animation in Good Photons" stops being a blocker because it's no longer in the loop.
The recursion-of-modulators, curves, ribbons, loops — none of that cares what renders it. It's generally useful, exactly as you suspected.

And here's the encouraging part: you're much closer than the scope suggests, because your substrate already exists. You have an expression VM (patternEval, the 78 POV functions, x y z r), an isosurface root-finder + mesher, material-props-by-function over UV/xyz, and camera curves with density/speed sub-curves. The animation layer is mostly two additions:
Thread a t variable through the VM (you already have x y z r; add t), then re-evaluate/re-mesh per frame.
One sweep/loft geometry primitive.

That's a small core for a huge expressive range.
Collapse the ribbon zoo into one primitive

All your ribbon variants — spine + rotation curve + width curve; spine + lateral rotation curves + distance curve; straight cross-links vs. curved "elongated blob"; closed cross-sections along a perpendicular frame — are one operation:

> sweep(spine, profile, frameField, scaleField, twistField) where every argument is an Animatable.
profile = two points → flat ribbon; profile = closed curve → tube/blob; profile = polygon → faceted.
straight cross-links vs. curved links = whether you connect corresponding profile samples with lines or splines.
width/distance/rotation curves are just scaleField / twistField / offsets, and each can be a curve or an expression (as you noted, they're especially amenable to math).

Build the sweep once; the whole zoo is parameter choices.
The modulate-the-modulators recursion is just a signal DAG

Define one uniform type — call it Animatable<T> — that is either a constant, a keyframed curve, an expression, or another node's output. Every modulator is a node that takes Animatable inputs and produces an Animatable output. Recursion (modulators of modulators of modulators…) falls out for free with zero special-casing — it's a DAG of signal nodes evaluated at time t. This is exactly the Houdini-CHOPs / demoscene / FRP model, and almost certainly the shape of what you did in scribbles3.
The frame-angle question (you flagged it twice)

You don't want an absolute angle per point — that's the wrong question and it's why it feels unanswerable. You want a consistent frame field along the curve, and the principled choice is a rotation-minimizing frame (RMF): transport an initial frame along the curve introducing zero extra twist. Compute it with the double-reflection method (Wang et al. 2008) — cheap, stable, and it doesn't flip at inflection points or blow up on straight segments the way a Frenet frame does. Now every point has a well-defined orientation without you ever choosing per-point angles.

For a closed loop, RMF around the loop lands back rotated by a residual angle Δ (the holonomy / accumulated twist — physically the writhe). To close seamlessly: distribute −Δ linearly over arclength (or solve a harmonic/energy-min distribution if you want it "elastic," which is likely your scribbles3 approach). Add integer multiples of 2π for intentional twist — a half-turn gives you a Möbius ribbon. That single Δ-compensation is the whole trick to "the angle through the last/first point."
Seamless time loops: make periodicity structural, not a fix-up

Rather than building a loop and patching the seam, parametrize time as an angle θ ∈ [0,2π) and build every signal from inherently periodic functions of θ — Fourier/epicycle sums Σ aₖcos(kθ)+bₖsin(kθ). Any closed loop is a sum of rotating circles (that's just its Fourier series). If every modulator is periodic with commensurate periods, the entire nested system loops with no seam by construction — no last-point-meets-first-point bookkeeping at all. Your "harmonic loops and loops of loops" is literally epicycles-on-epicycles, and it's seamless for free. This is the time analogue of the RMF principle: choose a representation where the property you want is guaranteed rather than repaired.

My recommendation on scope. The risk here is reinventing Houdini. Resist it. A minimal core captures ~80% of everything above:
Animatable<T> signal graph (constant / curve / expression / node-ref) with a t variable in the VM.
Curve primitive + RMF frame field + loop-closure twist compensation.
One sweep(spine, profile, frame, scale, twist) primitive.
Field-domain transforms (incl. N-D rotate-and-slice) + per-frame re-mesh via the isosurface machinery you already have.

That's genuinely small given what's already built, it's renderer-agnostic, and it's authored against the fast preview so Good Photons' cost never gates iteration. I'd call it worth it.
